The calendar systems established by Babylonians were based on natural cycles for agricultural coordination.

Proof exists, at least 5,000 years ago, and long before the advent of the Roman calendar, that humans began to measure time, introducing calendars to coordinate communal activities, trade, and particularly planting and harvesting.

They based their calendars on three natural cycles: the solar day, the lunar month, and the solar year.

Of the three, the moon had greater social impact for those living near the equator, where its waxing and waning was more conspicuous than the passing of the seasons.
