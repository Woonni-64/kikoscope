In a village in Guangxi, China, solar panels on rooftops power "all-electric homestays"; in Malaysia, data centers fund battery storage with savings from green electricity subsidies; at an IEA headquarters meeting, experts forecast a doubling of global renewable capacity by 2030. These 2025 scenes capture a pivotal shift in the global energy landscape: renewable energy is no longer a marginal supplement but a core pillar of the power system.

A core driver of 2025's renewable energy boom is the unstoppable growth of solar and wind power, fueled by cost declines and policy support. The IEA predicts that global renewable capacity will add 4,600 gigawatts (GW) between 2025 and 2030—equivalent to the combined capacity of China, the EU, and Japan today. Solar PV leads this surge, accounting for 80% of the projected growth, as its costs have plummeted by 90% over the past decade.

Beyond solar and wind, diversified renewable technologies are expanding the energy mix. In Binyang, 6 biomass hydrogen-rich gasification power stations are under construction, with a total capacity of 12 MW, expected to generate 288 million kWh of green electricity annually. A 2.5 MW waste incineration power project will process 800 tons of domestic waste daily, converting waste into 160 million kWh of electricity yearly.

Equally transformative is technological innovation solving the "intermittency puzzle" of renewables. Energy storage has emerged as a game-changer: China's Binyang County deployed the country's first distributed sodium-ion battery storage system, which boosts local renewable absorption and ensures stable supply. In Malaysia, data centers are reinvesting savings from green electricity into 1.5 gigawatt-hours (GWh) of battery storage to guarantee 24/7 green power for international clients.

Yet significant challenges remain, including grid integration bottlenecks and financing gaps. Even with rapid growth, connecting new renewables to outdated grids remains a hurdle: 20% of solar projects in India face delays due to transmission constraints. The divide between developed and developing nations is stark: low-income countries receive just 2% of global renewable investment, despite having abundant solar and wind resources.

The path forward requires coordinated action. Policy frameworks must evolve to prioritize grid modernization and remove regulatory barriers. Financial institutions need to develop innovative instruments—like green bonds and blended finance—to mobilize capital for developing nations. Technological collaboration should focus on next-generation solutions, from advanced storage to smart grid management.

As the world stands at this energy inflection point, the choices made today will determine whether the renewable transition delivers on its promise of a sustainable, equitable future. The momentum is building, but the hard work of implementation lies ahead.
