<p>The AFL has fined Carlton $75,000 for not removing Elijah Hollands from the field while he was experiencing a mental health episode during round six against Collingwood.</p> <p>Hollands appeared to be acting erratically during the match at the MCG, and was admitted to hospital for treatment several days later.</p> <p>The AFL found Carlton should have removed Hollands from the game earlier based on visual cues, performance data and the player’s previously disclosed mental health challenges, and that the club’s failure to do so brought the game into disrepute.</p> <aside class="element element-rich-link element--thumbnail"> <p> <span>Related: </span><a href="https://www.theguardian.com/sport/2026/apr/23/elijah-hollands-carlton-afl-player-coach-michael-voss-media">Carlton coach Michael Voss accuses media of bullying after Elijah Hollands incident</a> </p> </aside>  <p>The AFL’s chief executive, Andrew Dillon, said the “substantial sanction” was made against the club “collectively” rather than singling out any individuals.</p> <p>“What transpired during the game was something we haven’t seen before and while Elijah did undergo checks, he should not have been left on the field for that period of time,” Dillon said, adding that the incident had highlighted areas of improvement for the code.</p> <p>“Whilst our physical injury protocols are strong and well understood, mental health presentations can be variable and are complex.”</p> <p>In a statement, Carlton acknowledged the AFL’s finding that Hollands should have been removed from the field “sooner than the 20-minute mark of the final quarter” and the resulting fine, but did not admit fault.</p> <p>The club said “it supports its people, who are highly-respected professionals with the utmost integrity, and operate with genuine care for all players and staff who represent the football club”. </p> <p>“We accept the outcome in the interest of enabling all parties to move forward, while continuing to ensure the health and wellbeing of our people remains a priority,” Carlton chief executive, Graham Wright, said in the statement.</p> <p>The club cited the <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/sport/2026/apr/20/carlton-player-elijah-hollands-admitted-to-hospital-after-mental-health-episode">ongoing WorkSafe investigation</a> and Hollands’ privacy as reasons for not providing more information about the incident.</p>    <p>As a result of the inquiry, the AFL is implementing several measures to better equip the sport and its clubs to support the mental health of participants.</p> <p>Clubs have typically employed psychologists on a part-time basis, but they will now be required to employ a full-time psychologist.</p> <aside class="element element-rich-link element--thumbnail"> <p> <span>Related: </span><a href="https://www.theguardian.com/sport/blog/2026/may/04/the-swans-are-fast-bold-skilful-and-making-the-most-of-the-afls-new-rules-its-a-good-time-to-be-a-forward">The Swans are fast, bold, skilful – and making the most of the AFL’s new rules. It’s a good time to be a forward | Jonathan Horn</a> </p> </aside>  <p>A new wellbeing governance committee is being established, alongside new standards and guidelines, expanded mental health literacy and a review of football soft cap policies.</p> <p>“There are lessons in this for all of us,” the AFL’s head of health and football, Laura Kane, said.</p> <p>“The main one is that our duty of care must continue to evolve, and that must include mental health and wellbeing.”</p> <p>Kane said Hollands had contributed to the AFL’s review.</p> <p>“I won’t speak to the specifics of the how Elijah’s doing, but I will say that we’re really grateful for his contribution and that of his family to this inquiry,” she said.</p> <p>Although Blues coach Michael Voss defended his staff following the incident <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/sport/2026/apr/23/elijah-hollands-carlton-afl-player-coach-michael-voss-media">and accused the media of bullying</a>, Carlton has accepted the fine.
