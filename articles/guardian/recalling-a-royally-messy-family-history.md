<p>“Shamed by the Epstein scandal, riven by infighting: have the UK royals ever been in a bigger mess than this?” asks the headline on Stephen Bates’s article (<a href="https://www.theguardian.com/commentisfree/2026/feb/04/jeffrey-epstein-scandal-uk-royals-legacy-queen">6 February</a>). Just a brief look back into history: the civil war between Stephen and Matilda, the Wars of the Roses, Charles I losing the throne and his head, Edward VIII’s abdication…<br><strong>Dr Violet Moller</strong><br><em>Marston St Lawrence, Northamptonshire</em></p> <p>• I enjoyed Peter Bradshaw’s review of the excellent Ashes and Diamonds (<a href="https://www.theguardian.com/film/2026/feb/03/ashes-and-diamonds-review-andrzej-wajda-bleak-post-second-world-war-drama">3 February</a>). He quotes the film where Krystyna asks Maciek why he’s wearing dark glasses. His answer about it being a souvenir of unrequited love for the homeland is a bitter joke. The eyes of resistance fighters who’d spent months underground, hiding from the Nazis, during the Warsaw Uprising (as memorialised in the equally excellent <a href="https://player.bfi.org.uk/subscription/film/watch-kanal-1956-online">Kanal</a>) were too sensitive to endure sunlight when they returned at last to the open air.<br><strong>Michael Newton</strong><br><em>Rotterdam, the Netherlands</em></p> <p>• Re Deutsche Bahn’s family coaches (<a href="https://www.theguardian.com/environment/2026/feb/02/our-ducks-get-the-royal-treatment-too">Letters, 2 February</a>), in 1982, France’s SNCF introduced carriages with a children’s play area, including climbing frames, toys and other activities, and a nursery (<em>espace enfants</em>), on a number of long-distance services. There was even a <a href="https://www.ina.fr/ina-eclaire-actu/trains-corail-espaces-enfants-aire-de-jeu-sncf">promotional video</a>.<br><strong>Jeremy Nicholls</strong><br><em>Audlem, Cheshire</em></p> <p>• In 1964, my elder sister married a Norwegian and went to live in Norway. When my middle-aged parents went to visit, they were shocked at having to sleep under a “dyne”, pronounced “dee-na” (<a href="https://www.theguardian.com/science/2026/feb/05/lets-reach-out-to-american-english">Letters, 5 February</a>). They insisted&nbsp;on calling it a “downy”.<br><strong>Ursula Hutchinson</strong><br><em>Newport, Isle of Wight</em></p> <p>• In Australia, a duvet is known as&nbsp;a&nbsp;“doona”.
