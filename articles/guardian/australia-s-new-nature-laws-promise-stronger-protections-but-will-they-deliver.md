<p>Five years after an <a href="https://www.dcceew.gov.au/sites/default/files/documents/epbc-act-review-final-report-october-2020.pdf" rel="nofollow">independent review</a> found that Australia’s environmental laws were not fit for purpose, the federal government last year conducted an overhaul of legislation that it said would strengthen national nature protections.</p> <p>The reforms to the Environment Protection and Biodiversity Conservation Act included tighter laws around <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/australia-news/2025/nov/27/australia-nature-laws-labor-greens-deal-environment-protection-biodiversity-conservation-act">land clearing</a> and the establishment of a national environmental protection agency (EPA). They also promised new standards to better protect nature and Indigenous cultural heritage, and to improve First Nations peoples’ engagement in decision-making.</p> <p>The changes followed decades of advocacy from conservation groups, backed by strong public support. More than 50,000 Australians signed a World Wide Fund for Nature (WWF) <a href="https://wwf.org.au/get-involved/protect-aussie-wildlife/" rel="nofollow">petition</a> to improve nature laws, and hundreds of thousands more have called for reform through community groups and environmental campaigns.</p> <p>“These reforms show what’s possible when we put nature first,” says WWF-Australia’s chief executive officer, Dermot O’Gorman. “Closing destructive loopholes and creating a national EPA is a major step forward, giving nature a fighting chance.</p> <p>“But the job isn’t done. Australia needs strong, enforceable standards and clear limits on climate harm, with no new coal and gas project approvals, to protect our forests, wildlife and the reef. Now is the time to turn ambition into action and secure a nature-positive future for all.”</p> <p>The big question is: can the new laws actually deliver meaningful protection on the ground – and on country?</p> <h2>A tropical oasis under pressure</h2> <p>For Whitney Rassip, a Djiru traditional owner and the chief executive officer of the Girringun Aboriginal Corporation in far north Queensland, the answer begins at home. Djiru Country stretches from North Maria Creek to the Hull River in the south, taking in Mission Beach, Bingil Bay and offshore islands including Dunk and Bedarra. It is one of the few places in Australia where lowland rainforest meets the coral reef, and is home to the endangered cassowary.</p>    <ul> <li><p>A Cassowary at Birdworld Kuranda in North Queensland Australia. Image credit: © David Clode/ Unsplash</p></li> </ul> <p>“It’s a tropical oasis,” Rassip says. “Very tranquil, it’s relaxing, it’s peaceful.” But for traditional owners, this landscape also carries a far deeper meaning. “A lot of history has happened there since colonial invasion, but we’ve been able to retain some of our significant areas and stories,” Rassip says.</p> <p>“That cultural landscape makes it very important. That’s my father’s country, and his father’s country.”</p> <p>From that perspective, Rassip was pleased to see that the law reforms included stronger federal protections against <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/wwf-australia-we-all-need-trees/2025/oct/01/what-native-forest-logging-is-really-costing-nsw">land clearing</a>, which come into effect in 2027. “Even now, we’re still seeing a lot of the lowland rainforest areas being cleared across all different land tenures,” she says. Littoral rainforest, a <a href="https://www.dcceew.gov.au/sites/default/files/documents/littoral-rainforest.pdf" rel="nofollow">critically endangered</a> coastal ecosystem, is at particular risk, especially on private land. “That’s really concerning.”</p> <h2>Nature needs strong action</h2> <p>Advocates say the real test of the reforms will be their implementation, particularly whether the new environmental standards actually define how to protect nature and whether the EPA is given the resources and authority needed to enforce them.</p> <p>Adam Muir, WWF-Australia’s nature positive policy manager, says the new regulator needs to be properly equipped to succeed. “I’m concerned that the new national EPA may be established by simply shifting existing departmental resources to the new agency,” he says. “It needs additional funding and a different culture to operate as a modern, best-practice regulator.</p> <p>“The draft environmental standards we’ve seen do not yet provide the clear rules needed to protect nature, and help developers know what they can and can’t do.”</p> <p>Rassip says stronger enforcement could benefit both biodiversity and cultural heritage. “Non-Indigenous legislation separates environmental and cultural heritage laws into completely different things,” she says. “But to us, they’re one and the same.”</p> <p>Meaningful reform, Rassip says, must also include traditional owners in decision-making. “These laws are beneficial to nature and the environment,” she says. “But it’s important to remember that it’s also our cultural landscape.”</p> <p><em><strong> <a href="https://wwf.org.au/get-involved/protect-aussie-wildlife/?utm_source=guardian&amp;utm_medium=blog_link&amp;utm_campaign=eng_nature_laws&amp;utm_term=broad&amp;utm_content=nature_laws_advertorial_hyperlink" rel="nofollow">Join WWF-Australia’s call to turn our ambitious new nature laws into action.
