<p>Donald Trump, who recently posted an image on social media which portrayed him as Jesus Christ (<a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/apr/15/donald-trump-ai-images-jesus">or, rather, “a doctor”</a>), and who seems unable to stop attacking the pope, read the Bible to America on Tuesday night.</p> <p>Sitting behind his desk in the Oval Office, hands resting on a book that looked like a Bible, Trump stared straight into the camera (presumably there was a teleprompter) <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=7oqyMMBAapY">as he recited from the book 2 Chronicles</a>. It’s a passage which has become fashionable among the right wing, and which quotes God as saying:</p> <p>“If my people, which are called by my name, shall humble themselves, and pray, and seek my face, and turn from their wicked ways; then will I hear from heaven, and will forgive their sin, and will heal their land.”</p> <aside class="element element-rich-link element--thumbnail"> <p> <span>Related: </span><a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/apr/21/trump-bible-passage-oval-office">Trump reads from Bible in Oval Office in taped message for Christian group</a> </p> </aside>  <p>Trump didn’t actually read it to the whole of the US, of course. Instead he read it, in a <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/apr/21/trump-bible-passage-oval-office">taped message</a> that was broadcast via a slightly janky livestream, to anyone enthusiastic enough to tune into a weeklong, rightwing, “America Reads the Bible” event where 500 people – a collection of true believers, Republican politicians, grifters, weirdos and homophobes – are taking it in turns to read the Bible cover to cover.</p> <p>To some, Trump reading scripture may have seemed a little cynical. Trump is desperate to win back angry Christians after he alienated many with that <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2026/apr/13/trump-ai-image-christ-like-figure-backlash">Son of God AI photo</a> , and with <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/world/2026/apr/13/donald-trump-pope-leo">the attacks on Pope Leo</a> (among other things, Trump accused God’s representative on Earth of being “soft on crime”).</p> <p>People might also fairly question the sincerity of Trump’s effort, given the president’s historically tenuous relationship with the Christian faith.</p> <p>Trump says the Bible is his favorite book, but during his 2016 campaign for president, he was <a href="https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=10qbt0LHmvE">unable to name</a> a single Bible verse. As he sought to win over evangelical voters, he referred to the communion as “my little wine” and “my little cracker”.</p> <p>In a church visit around the same time, Trump <a href="https://www.washingtonpost.com/national/religion/clarification-trump-puts-cash-in-communion-plate/2016/02/01/65f8c67a-c937-11e5-b9ab-26591104bb19_story.html">dumped cash on to a plate</a> that was meant to hold the communion.</p> <p>He has also said he has never asked God for forgiveness, which is kind of a key point within Christianity, while at an event at religious university, he referred to the Bible book “Second Corinthians” as “Two Corinthians”.</p> <p>Trump also opted not to place his hand on the Bible during his 2024 inauguration, which is hardly the worst act of his second term, but it <a href="https://abcnews.com/Politics/trump-inauguration-hand-bible-swearing-in/story?id=117897715">upset people all the same</a>.</p> <p>It’s hard to say what the impact of Trump’s Bible-reading will be: partly because it is hard to believe many people have been closely following America Reads the Bible, an event organized by a woman whose name is Bunni Pounds, who claims “God called” her to be a “missionary to America”.</p>    <p>Pounds is the founder and vice-president of an organization called Christians Engaged, a get out the (Christian nationalist) vote group which also offers a line in homophobia: <a href="https://christiansengaged.org/blog/identity-in-christ#:~:text=Here%20is%20the%20difference.%20Someone,22%3A15%20to%20name%20a%20few">one article</a> on its website refers to homosexuality and gay marriage as “abominations”.</p> <p>Indeed, the list of readers is rife with those who wield their faith like a weapon. Mike Johnson, the US House speaker who once <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/2023/nov/06/speaker-mike-johnson-covenant-eyes-app-quitting-porn">used an anti-porn app to monitor his and his son’s internet activity</a>, is among them, along with Pete Hegseth, the <a href="https://www.theguardian.com/us-news/ng-interactive/2026/apr/10/pete-hegseth-christianity-iran-war-crusade">Bible-thumping</a>, hawkish defense secretary.</p> <p>Others include a pastor at the Christian ministry Faith and Liberty, which last year lobbied against a bill which would have introduced anti-discrimination protections for LGBTQ+ people. The ministry <a href="https://faithandlibertydc.org/FLemail/fl-ethankyoudonate/efl2102faxea?t=EFL2102-FaxEA&amp;c=EFL2102-FaxEA">complained</a> that the legislation would aid what it described as “the obscenities and recruiting efforts of the LGBTQ agenda”. “America the Brave will turn into ‘America the Queer’,” the organization concluded. </p> <p>There were also speakers from Her Voice Movement, which claims people can help avert an LGBTQ+ “crisis” by becoming a “visionary” member of its organization. It costs $4,000 a month to be a visionary member, but in exchange people get an ebook and a T-shirt.</p> <p>Religious scholars have been less than impressed. Jemar Tisby, a historian who writes about racial justice and faith, wrote: “You cannot quote the Bible while justifying violence, war and exclusion.”</p> <p>Pastor Doug Pagitt, executive director of the progressive group Vote Common Good, felt similarly. He told Associated Press in a statement: “If you like reading the Bible, try living it.”</p>
