<p>There’s plenty to see at Parrtjima in 2026, and even more to do. At this free 10-night festival returning to Mparntwe (Alice Springs) from 10-19 April, connecting to country and Aboriginal culture doesn’t come only from watching and listening. It also comes from doing: drawing, weaving, tasting, dancing and speaking side by side with the people who carry these stories.</p> <p>Held outdoors at Alice Springs Desert Park, the festival draws people from Aboriginal communities in the Northern Territory and beyond, along with visitors from across Australia and overseas.</p> <p>Parrtjima’s cultural adviser, Paul Ah Chee, expects this year’s festival to be “absolutely fantastic”. The interactive program, developed in consultation with traditional owners and artists, is designed not only to connect visitors to the living landscape, he says, but to bring people from all backgrounds together to deepen their understanding of Aboriginal culture.</p> <h2>More than words</h2> <p>Language is Parrtjima’s guiding theme this year, and the festival promises cultural illumination and participation, inviting listening, learning and connection through art, culture and story.</p> <p>“Language is more than the spoken word,” says Ah Chee, who lives in Alice Springs and is of Yankunytjatjara, Wangkangurru and Arrernte descent. “It can come in all different shapes and forms and gives you a sense of belonging. It can be spoken; it can be symbols or signs – people used signs a lot, especially when they were hunting – and if you told a story, you had to sing it, dance it, speak it, and you had to paint it or draw it.”</p>    <p>Across the festival, this expanded understanding of language will be brought to life through dynamic light installations and interactive experiences that honour language in all its forms. Artworks will illuminate the stories, rhythms and beauty of language, from ancient knowledge to contemporary expression, revealing how it carries memory, meaning and connection across generations.</p> <p>Visitors will learn some Arrernte words through workshops and interactive displays. <em>Kwatye</em> (pronounced kwat-jar) means both water and rain. <em>Werte </em>(pronounced woord-da) means hi or hello.</p> <h2>Stepping into story</h2> <p>One of Parrtjima’s most popular experiences, Grounded, will return this year. The installation projects illuminated artworks directly onto the ground, turning the red earth into a living canvas. You don’t just look at Grounded; you step into it, becoming part of the artwork as it shifts beneath your feet.</p>    <p>“The atmosphere is amazing; everyone’s just immersed,” Ah Chee says. “People gather around it and walk on top of the art. Kids are kicking up dust. Everyone’s part of it and, honestly, it’s a pleasure to watch.</p> <p>“It’s one of those places where everybody’s equal. People are just walking around, sharing the experience. There’s no division. Everyone’s in the same space.”</p> <p>While the large-scale light installations draw people in visually, Parrtjima’s heart lies equally in storytelling: passing on knowledge, strengthening connection and inviting understanding. Stories will be shared during the festival’s interactive workshops, which will run nightly alongside markets, food stalls, performances and exhibitions.</p> <h2>Learning by doing</h2> <p>On illuminated sandboxes, artists will create sand paintings while telling stories, explaining symbols, inviting questions and encouraging participation. “It’s very much inclusive,” Ah Chee says.</p> <p>Aboriginal people have been telling stories and passing on knowledge through sand drawings for more than 65,000 years; it’s part of what makes Aboriginal culture the oldest continuing living culture on Earth.</p> <p>“That’s the thing with Aboriginal design and art,” Ah Chee says. “It comes from somewhere – from generations and generations and generations.”</p> <p>Bush food and medicine demonstrations will offer tastings alongside explanations of how particular plants are used for food, healing and wellbeing. Watercolour workshops run by local Aboriginal art centres will give participants a chance to learn techniques and how to interpret meanings from artists, while soft-sculpture workshops will invite children and adults alike to create woven puppets and sculptural forms using textiles. Screen-printing workshops will add another tactile layer.</p> <p>“It’s all about being creative,” Ah Chee says. “Using your hands. Using all your senses.”</p> <h2>Coming together</h2> <p>The festival also serves as a vital gathering place for Aboriginal people, strengthening pride, identity and connection in their communities.</p> <p>For the artists, storytellers and musicians, Parrtjima is both a platform and a gathering point. As well as travelling from remote communities to take part, established local performers return year after year to share their work with audiences at home.</p>    <p>“It’s an opportunity to showcase creativity, but also to show brotherhood and sisterhood, to share experiences and positivity and a sense of hope,” Ah Chee says.</p> <p>Music plays a key role in that message. Ah Chee will perform alongside his son Roland at Parrtjima 2026. Other artists will include Electric Fields, Emma Donovan and Drifting Clouds.</p> <p>“My music is about humanity,” Ah Chee says. “Sharing, caring, understanding – being true to who you are. I have a line in one of my songs that says: ‘You don’t have to be loud to stand and be proud of who you are.’”</p> <p>That sense of pride is especially important for younger generations, Ah Chee says, explaining that he hopes Parrtjima inspires them to hold onto their culture, “to say I’m Aboriginal, and I’m proud of being Aboriginal”.</p> <p>Talks and speaker sessions appear on the program, but visitors can expect conversations rather than lectures.</p> <p>Parrtjima’s In Conversation sessions invite audiences to sit, listen and reflect, bringing together artists, performers, elders and cultural leaders to share the stories behind their work and explore ideas at the heart of culture and country.</p> <p>“That’s how trust and understanding is built, through conversation,” Ah Chee says.</p> <p><em><strong><a href="https://parrtjimaaustralia.com.au/" rel="nofollow">Register for free now to experience Parrtjima</a></strong></em><strong>.</strong></p>
